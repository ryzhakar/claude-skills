# Core Points: manifesto-writing

## Iteration 1

**Point:** Manifestos declare and command through compression plus conviction, transforming explanatory source material by stripping hedging and building urgency rather than accommodating objections.

**Evidence:**
- Lines 19-25: "A manifesto is not a lecture. It declares. It commands. It names enemies. It assumes the reader is ready to be changed... Manifestos state positions as facts, strip hedging, build urgency, demand action. The transformation is compression plus conviction."
- Lines 62-63: "State positions. Do not justify them at length. The manifesto assumes the reader is ready to hear. If they need convincing, this is the wrong document."
- Lines 39: "Hedging language ('I think,' 'perhaps,' 'it seems,' 'sort of')" listed as what to cut

## Iteration 2

**Point:** Every manifesto must name the enemy early and make stakes clear, establishing what it opposes in the first section.

**Evidence:**
- Lines 57-59: "Name the Enemy Early — Every manifesto opposes something. Name it in the first section. Make the stakes clear."
- Lines 78-79: "End with a Choice — Manifestos call readers to decide. The closing should make the choice stark."
- Line 134: "Find the enemy — what does this oppose?" as a core process step

## Iteration 3

**Point:** Use imperative commands and active constructions, never suggestions or academic hedging, converting "you might consider Y" to simply "Y."

**Evidence:**
- Lines 65-67: "Commands, Not Suggestions — Use imperative mood. 'Do X.' Not 'You should consider doing X.' Not 'It might be helpful to do X.'"
- Lines 46-54: Table showing transformation patterns like "'I believe that X' → 'X.'" and "'You might consider Y' → 'Y.' (imperative)"
- Lines 137: "Convert to commands — change suggestions to imperatives" as dedicated process step

## Iteration 4

**Point:** Preserve core distinctions, sharp vocabulary, and memorable aphorisms while cutting audience interaction, extended examples, repetition, and explanatory scaffolding.

**Evidence:**
- Lines 29-34: "What to Preserve" lists "Core distinctions — the conceptual hinges the argument turns on", "Sharp vocabulary — terms the author coined or defined precisely", "Memorable aphorisms — lines that crystallize ideas"
- Lines 37-42: "What to Cut" includes "Audience interaction, jokes, asides", "Extended examples and digressions", "Repetition for emphasis (works in speech, bloats on page)", "Explanatory scaffolding"
- Line 138: "Tighten — cut words, cut sentences, cut sections until it hurts"

## Iteration 5

**Point:** Manifestos are tools for crystallization and transmission to sympathetic audiences, not for discovery, nuance preservation, or persuasion from first principles.

**Evidence:**
- Lines 145-152: "When Not to Write a Manifesto" section stating "When the source material has no clear position (manifestos require conviction)", "When nuance is the point (manifestos flatten nuance)", "When the audience needs convincing from first principles (manifestos assume sympathy)", "A manifesto is a tool for crystallization and transmission, not for discovery or persuasion from scratch."
- Line 63: "The manifesto assumes the reader is ready to hear. If they need convincing, this is the wrong document."
- Line 19: "It assumes the reader is ready to be changed."

## Rank Summary

1. **Compression plus conviction transformation** — The fundamental operation: converting explanatory material into declarative commands by stripping hedging and building urgency
2. **Name the enemy early** — Structural requirement to establish opposition and stakes in the opening section
3. **Imperative commands over suggestions** — Tone and voice requirement using active imperatives rather than academic hedging
4. **Preserve distinctions, cut scaffolding** — Content selection criteria for what to keep (core ideas, vocabulary, aphorisms) versus what to remove (examples, repetition, explanations)
5. **Crystallization for sympathetic audiences** — Scope limitation defining when manifestos work (sympathetic readers) versus when they fail (nuance, discovery, first-principles persuasion)
